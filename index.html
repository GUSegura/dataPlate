Adapting the student meal plan to the needs of the Colombian household through a nutritional lens

In 2022, the World Food Program (WFP) conducted a food security and nutrition assessment in Colombia. Findings from the report stated that it is critical to maintain and expand emergency assistance to meet the food security needs of the populations identified. The WFP recommended that such assistance should incorporate a nutritional lens to make sure vulnerable groups are able to access a nutritious diet. In this data visualization, we take a look at the food security of households with children, focusing on nutrition.

7,097 Colombian households were surveyed

Our data comes from the Colombian Emergency Food Security and Nutrition Assessment (EFSA).

For more information from the World Food Programme (WFP), click here.

Of these 7,097 households, 47% were households with students

49% of the households with students relied on the school meal plan

The school meal plan is known as the Programa de Alimentaci√≥n Escolar (PAE). In 2018, the school meal program consisted of in-school meals provided five times per week for the ten months of the school year.

For more information on the PAE from the Global Child Nutrition Foundation (GCNF), click here.

In total, 1 out of every 5 Colombian households participated in the school meal plan.

Examining access to quality food and the nutrition of this population provides us with insights into the effectiveness of the school meal plan.

As the number of students in a household increased, so did the proportion of students on the meal plan.

Families with more children are generally more reliant on school meal plans.

On average, among families with 7 children, 82.14% were on the meal plan (versus 48.13% for single-child households).

However, meal plans do not completely alleviate families from the financial burden of nutrition.

Families often turn to coping strategies for further financial assistance.

As the number of children increases, there is a general increased use of coping strategies. On average, families with 7 children reduced adult food consumption 4 of the past 7 days so that children could eat.

How can the meal plan be improved?

We can consider the quality of the diet of meal plan households.

The food consumption score (FCS) is an indicator of diet diversity, meal frequency, and nutritional value.

This index can be used to calculate household food consumption status.

Households status may be evaluated as acceptable, borderline, or poor.

We can break down consumption by food groups and FCS status to further analyze household nutrition.

Diets appear unbalanced regardless of FCS status.

Even in meal-plan households with 'acceptable' consumption, many vital food groups are absent.

65% of all households eat vegetables less than twice a week.

In Colombia, the average adult over 20 years old consumes only 32% of the recommended vegetable intake.

Despite 12.7% of children suffering from malnutrition, the degree to which children meet the recommended vegetable intake, either through school or household meals, is largely missing from the literature.

One way to mitigate food insecurity among Colombian youth is by reforming the school meal plans.

In doing so, we can address nutritional deficiencies among food groups like vegetables, and also reduce the financial burden and coping strategies households undergo in providing food.
